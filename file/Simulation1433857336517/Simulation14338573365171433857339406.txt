			<h1 id="firstHeading" class="firstHeading" lang="en">Synthetic biology</h1>
									<div id="bodyContent" class="mw-body-content">
									<div id="siteSub">From Wikipedia, the free encyclopedia</div>
								<div id="contentSub"></div>
												<div id="jump-to-nav" class="mw-jump">
					Jump to:					<a href="#mw-head">navigation</a>, 					<a href="#p-search">search</a>
				</div>
				<div id="mw-content-text" lang="en" dir="ltr" class="mw-content-ltr"><p><b>Synthetic biology</b> is an interdisciplinary branch of <a href="/wiki/Biology" title="Biology">biology</a>, combining disciplines such as <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Evolutionary_biology" title="Evolutionary biology">evolutionary biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Molecular_biology" title="Molecular biology">molecular biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Systems_biology" title="Systems biology">systems biology</a>, <a href="/wiki/Biophysics" title="Biophysics">biophysics</a>, <a href="/wiki/Electrical_engineering" title="Electrical engineering">electrical engineering</a>, and is in many ways related to <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a>.</p>
<p>The definition of synthetic biology is heavily debated not only among natural scientists but also in the <a href="/wiki/Human_sciences" title="Human sciences" class="mw-redirect">human sciences</a>, <a href="/wiki/Arts" title="Arts" class="mw-redirect">arts</a> and politics.<sup id="cite_ref-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-1"><span>[</span>1<span>]</span></a></sup> One popular definition is "designing and constructing <a href="/wiki/BioBrick" title="BioBrick">biological devices</a><sup id="cite_ref-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-2"><span>[</span>2<span>]</span></a></sup> and <a href="/wiki/Biological_systems" title="Biological systems" class="mw-redirect">biological systems</a> for useful purposes." However, the functional aspects of this definition stem from molecular biology and biotechnology.<sup id="cite_ref-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-3"><span>[</span>3<span>]</span></a></sup></p>

<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="History">History</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=1" title="Edit section: History">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>The term "synthetic biology" has a history spanning the twentieth century. The first use was in <a href="/wiki/St%C3%A9phane_Leduc" title="Stéphane Leduc">Stéphane Leduc</a>’s publication of «&#160;Théorie physico-chimique de la vie et générations spontanées&#160;» (1910)<sup id="cite_ref-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-4"><span>[</span>4<span>]</span></a></sup> and «&#160;La Biologie Synthétique&#160;» (1912).<sup id="cite_ref-5" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-5"><span>[</span>5<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="noprint Inline-Template" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citing_sources#In-text_attribution" title="Wikipedia:Citing sources"><span title="The text in the vicinity of this tag needs attribution">who said this?</span></a></i>]</sup> In 1974, the Polish geneticist <a href="/wiki/Wac%C5%82aw_Szybalski" title="Wacław Szybalski">Wacław Szybalski</a> used the term "synthetic biology",<sup id="cite_ref-6" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-6"><span>[</span>6<span>]</span></a></sup> writing:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>Let me now comment on the question "what next". Up to now we are working on the descriptive phase of molecular biology. ... But the real challenge will start when we enter the synthetic phase of research in our field. We will then devise new control elements and add these new modules to the existing genomes or build up wholly new genomes. This would be a field with an unlimited expansion potential and hardly any limitations to building "new better control circuits" or ..... finally other "synthetic" organisms, like a "new better mouse". ... I am not concerned that we will run out of exciting and novel ideas, ... in the synthetic biology, in general.</p>
</blockquote>
<p>When in 1978 the <a href="/wiki/Nobel_Prize_in_Physiology_or_Medicine" title="Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine">Nobel Prize in Physiology or Medicine</a> was awarded to <a href="/wiki/Werner_Arber" title="Werner Arber">Arber</a>, <a href="/wiki/Daniel_Nathans" title="Daniel Nathans">Nathans</a> and <a href="/wiki/Hamilton_O._Smith" title="Hamilton O. Smith">Smith</a> for the discovery of <a href="/wiki/Restriction_enzyme" title="Restriction enzyme">restriction enzymes</a>, Wacław Szybalski wrote in an editorial comment in the journal <i><a href="/wiki/Gene_(journal)" title="Gene (journal)">Gene</a></i>:</p>
<blockquote>
<p>The work on restriction nucleases not only permits us easily to construct recombinant DNA molecules and to analyze individual genes, but also has led us into the new era of synthetic biology where not only existing genes are described and analyzed but also new gene arrangements can be constructed and evaluated.<sup id="cite_ref-7" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-7"><span>[</span>7<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
</blockquote>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Perspectives">Perspectives</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=2" title="Edit section: Perspectives">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Engineering">Engineering</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=3" title="Edit section: Engineering">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Engineers view biology as a <i>technology</i> – the <i>systems <a href="/wiki/Biotechnology" title="Biotechnology">biotechnology</a></i> or <i>systems <a href="/wiki/Biological_engineering" title="Biological engineering">biological engineering</a></i>.<sup id="cite_ref-8" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-8"><span>[</span>8<span>]</span></a></sup> Synthetic Biology includes the broad redefinition and expansion of biotechnology, with the ultimate goals of being able to design and build engineered biological systems that process information, manipulate chemicals, fabricate materials and structures, produce energy, provide food, and maintain and enhance human health (see <a href="/wiki/Biomedical_Engineering" title="Biomedical Engineering" class="mw-redirect">Biomedical Engineering</a>) and our environment.<sup id="cite_ref-9" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-9"><span>[</span>9<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>Studies in synthetic biology can be subdivided into broad classifications according to the approach they take to the problem at hand: standardization of biological parts, biomolecular engineering, genome engineering. Biomolecular engineering includes approaches which aim to create a toolkit of functional units that can be introduced to present new orthogonal functions in living cells. <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">Genetic engineering</a> includes approaches to construct synthetic chromosomes for whole or minimal organisms. Biomolecular design refers to the general idea of the de novo design and combination of biomolecular components. The task of each of these approaches is similar: To create a more synthetic entry at a higher level of complexity by manipulating a part of the preceding level.<sup id="cite_ref-10" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-10"><span>[</span>10<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Re-writing">Re-writing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=4" title="Edit section: Re-writing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Re-writers are synthetic biologists who are interested in testing the idea that since natural biological systems are so complicated, we would be better off re-building the natural systems that we care about, from the ground up, in order to provide engineered surrogates that are easier to understand and interact with.<sup id="cite_ref-11" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-11"><span>[</span>11<span>]</span></a></sup> Re-writers draw inspiration from <a href="/wiki/Refactoring" title="Refactoring" class="mw-redirect">refactoring</a>, a process sometimes used to improve computer software.</p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Key_enabling_technologies">Key enabling technologies</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=5" title="Edit section: Key enabling technologies">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>There are several key enabling technologies that are critical to the growth of synthetic biology. The key concepts include <a href="/wiki/Standardization" title="Standardization">standardization</a> of biological parts and hierarchical abstraction to permit using those parts in increasingly complex synthetic systems.<sup id="cite_ref-12" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-12"><span>[</span>12<span>]</span></a></sup> Achieving this is greatly aided by basic technologies of reading and writing of DNA (sequencing and fabrication), which are improving in price/performance exponentially <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://www.kurzweilai.net/articles/art0134.html">(Kurzweil 2001)</a>. Measurements under a variety of conditions are needed for accurate modeling and computer-aided-design (CAD).</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Standardized_DNA_parts">Standardized DNA parts</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=6" title="Edit section: Standardized DNA parts">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>The most used<sup id="cite_ref-primer_13-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-primer-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:22–23</sup> standardized DNA parts are <a href="/wiki/BioBrick" title="BioBrick">BioBrick</a> plasmids invented by <a href="/wiki/Tom_Knight_(scientist)" title="Tom Knight (scientist)">Tom Knight</a> in 2003.<sup id="cite_ref-14" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-14"><span>[</span>14<span>]</span></a></sup> Biobricks are stored at the <a href="/wiki/Registry_of_Standard_Biological_Parts" title="Registry of Standard Biological Parts">Registry of Standard Biological Parts</a> in Cambridge, Massachusetts and the BioBrick standard has been used by thousands of students worldwide in the <a href="/wiki/International_Genetically_Engineered_Machine" title="International Genetically Engineered Machine">international Genetically Engineered Machine</a> (iGEM) competition.<sup id="cite_ref-primer_13-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-primer-13"><span>[</span>13<span>]</span></a></sup><sup class="reference" style="white-space:nowrap;">:22–23</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_synthesis">DNA synthesis</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=7" title="Edit section: DNA synthesis">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>In 2007 it was reported that several companies were offering the <a href="/wiki/Gene_synthesis" title="Gene synthesis" class="mw-redirect">synthesis of genetic sequences</a> up to 2000 bp long, for a price of about $1 per base pair and a turnaround time of less than two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-15" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-15"><span>[</span>15<span>]</span></a></sup> Oligonucleotides harvested from a photolithographic or inkjet manufactured <a href="/wiki/DNA_chip" title="DNA chip" class="mw-redirect">DNA chip</a> combined with DNA mismatch error-correction allows inexpensive large-scale changes of <a href="/wiki/Codons" title="Codons" class="mw-redirect">codons</a> in genetic systems to improve <a href="/wiki/Gene_expression" title="Gene expression">gene expression</a> or incorporate novel amino-acids (see <a href="/wiki/George_M._Church" title="George M. Church">George M. Church</a>'s and <a href="/w/index.php?title=Anthony_Forster_(geneticist)&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Anthony Forster (geneticist) (page does not exist)">Anthony Forster</a>'s <a rel="nofollow" class="external text" href="http://arep.med.harvard.edu/SBP">synthetic cell projects</a>.)<sup id="cite_ref-16" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-16"><span>[</span>16<span>]</span></a></sup> This favors a synthesis-from-scratch approach.</p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="DNA_sequencing">DNA sequencing</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=8" title="Edit section: DNA sequencing">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p><a href="/wiki/DNA_sequencing" title="DNA sequencing">DNA sequencing</a> is determining the order of the <a href="/wiki/Nucleotide" title="Nucleotide">nucleotide</a> bases in a molecule of <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a>. Synthetic biologists make use of DNA sequencing in their work in several ways. First, large-scale genome sequencing efforts continue to provide a wealth of information on naturally occurring organisms. This information provides a rich substrate from which synthetic biologists can construct parts and devices. Second, synthetic biologists use sequencing to verify that they fabricated their engineered system as intended. Third, fast, cheap and reliable sequencing can also facilitate rapid detection and identification of synthetic systems and organisms.<sup id="cite_ref-17" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-17"><span>[</span>17<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Modeling">Modeling</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=9" title="Edit section: Modeling">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Models inform the design of engineered biological systems by allowing synthetic biologists to better predict system behavior prior to fabrication. Synthetic biology will benefit from better models of how biological molecules bind substrates and catalyze reactions, how DNA encodes the information needed to specify the cell and how multi-component integrated systems behave. Recently, multiscale models of gene regulatory networks have been developed that focus on synthetic biology applications. Simulations have been used that model all biomolecular interactions in transcription, translation, regulation, and induction of gene regulatory networks, guiding the design of synthetic systems.<sup id="cite_ref-18" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-18"><span>[</span>18<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Examples">Examples</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=10" title="Edit section: Examples">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_DNA">Synthetic DNA</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=11" title="Edit section: Synthetic DNA">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Artificial_gene_synthesis" title="Artificial gene synthesis">Artificial gene synthesis</a> and <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_genomics" title="Synthetic genomics">Synthetic genomics</a></div>
<p>Driven by dramatic decreases in costs of making <a href="/wiki/Oligonucleotides" title="Oligonucleotides" class="mw-redirect">oligonucleotides</a> ("oligos"), the sizes of DNA constructions from oligos have increased to the genomic level.<sup id="cite_ref-19" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-19"><span>[</span>19<span>]</span></a></sup> For example, in 2000, researchers at Washington University reported synthesis of the 9.6 kbp (kilo base pair) Hepatitis C virus genome from chemically synthesized 60 to 80-mers.<sup id="cite_ref-20" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-20"><span>[</span>20<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2002 researchers at <a href="/wiki/SUNY_Stony_Brook" title="SUNY Stony Brook" class="mw-redirect">SUNY Stony Brook</a> succeeded in synthesizing the 7741 base <a href="/wiki/Poliovirus" title="Poliovirus">poliovirus</a> genome from its published sequence, producing the second synthetic genome. This took about two years of work.<sup id="cite_ref-21" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-21"><span>[</span>21<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2003 the 5386 bp genome of the <a href="/wiki/Bacteriophage" title="Bacteriophage">bacteriophage</a> <a href="/wiki/Phi_X_174" title="Phi X 174">Phi X 174</a> was assembled in about two weeks.<sup id="cite_ref-22" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-22"><span>[</span>22<span>]</span></a></sup> In 2006, the same team, at the <a href="/wiki/J._Craig_Venter_Institute" title="J. Craig Venter Institute">J. Craig Venter Institute</a>, had constructed and patented a synthetic genome of a novel minimal bacterium, <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_laboratorium" title="Mycoplasma laboratorium">Mycoplasma laboratorium</a></i> and were working on getting it functioning in a living cell.<sup id="cite_ref-23" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-23"><span>[</span>23<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-24" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-24"><span>[</span>24<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_life">Synthetic life</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=12" title="Edit section: Synthetic life">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>One important topic in synthetic biology is <i>synthetic life</i>, that is, <a href="/wiki/Artificial_life" title="Artificial life">artificial life</a> created <i><a href="/wiki/In_vitro" title="In vitro">in vitro</a></i> from <a href="/wiki/Biochemical" title="Biochemical" class="mw-redirect">biochemicals</a> and their component materials. Synthetic life experiments attempt to either probe the origins of life, study some of the properties of life, or more ambitiously to recreate life from non-alive (<a href="/wiki/Abiotic" title="Abiotic" class="mw-redirect">abiotic</a>) substances. In May 2010, <a href="/wiki/Craig_Venter" title="Craig Venter">Craig Venter</a>'s group announced they had been able to assemble a complete genome of millions of base pairs, insert it into a cell, and cause that cell to start replicating.<sup id="cite_ref-25" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-25"><span>[</span>25<span>]</span></a></sup> For the creation of this semi-synthetic cell, first the complete DNA sequence of the genome of a bacterium <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_mycoides" title="Mycoplasma mycoides">Mycoplasma mycoides</a></i> was determined. A new genome was then designed based on this genome with watermarks and elements necessary for growth in yeast and genome transplantation added, as well as part of its sequence deliberately deleted. This new genome was synthesized in small fragments —over a thousand overlapping cassettes of synthetic oligonucleotides were created— which were then assembled in steps in yeast and other cells, and the complete genome was finally transplanted into a living cell from another species <i><a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_capricolum" title="Mycoplasma capricolum">Mycoplasma capricolum</a></i> from which all genetic material had been removed.<sup id="cite_ref-26" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-26"><span>[</span>26<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-venter_27-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venter-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> The cell divided and was "entirely controlled by (the) new genome", ultimately demonstrating that <a href="/wiki/DNA" title="DNA">DNA</a> can be very practically described by its chemical properties.<sup id="cite_ref-venter_27-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venter-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> This cell has been referred to by Venter as the "first synthetic cell", and was created at a cost of over $40 million.<sup id="cite_ref-venter_27-2" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venter-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> There is some debate within the scientific community over whether this cell can be considered completely synthetic on the grounds that:<sup id="cite_ref-venter_27-3" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venter-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> the chemically synthesized genome was an almost 1:1 copy of a naturally occurring genome and, the recipient cell was a naturally occurring bacterium. The Craig Venter Institute maintains the term "synthetic bacterial cell" but they also clarify "...we do not consider this to be "creating life from scratch" but rather we are creating new life out of already existing life using synthetic DNA." <sup id="cite_ref-28" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-28"><span>[</span>28<span>]</span></a></sup> Venter plans to patent his experimental cells, stating that "they are pretty clearly human inventions".<sup id="cite_ref-venter_27-4" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-venter-27"><span>[</span>27<span>]</span></a></sup> Its creators suggests that building 'synthetic life' would allow researchers to learn about life by building it, rather than by tearing it apart. They also propose to stretch the boundaries between life and machines until the two overlap to yield "truly programmable organisms."<sup id="cite_ref-29" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-29"><span>[</span>29<span>]</span></a></sup> Researchers involved stated that the creation of "true synthetic biochemical life" is relatively close in reach with current technology and cheap compared to the effort needed to place a man on the Moon.<sup id="cite_ref-30" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-30"><span>[</span>30<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Information_Storage">Information Storage</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=13" title="Edit section: Information Storage">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Scientists can encode vast amounts of digital information onto a single strand of <a href="/wiki/Synthetic_DNA" title="Synthetic DNA" class="mw-redirect">synthetic DNA</a>. In 2012, <a href="/wiki/George_M._Church" title="George M. Church">George M. Church</a> encoded one of his books about synthetic biology in DNA. The 5.3 <a href="/wiki/Megabit" title="Megabit">Mb</a> of data from the book is more than 1000 times greater than the previous largest amount of information to be stored in synthesized DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-31" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-31"><span>[</span>31<span>]</span></a></sup> A similar project had encoded the complete sonnets of William Shakespeare in DNA.<sup id="cite_ref-32" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-32"><span>[</span>32<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Synthetic_genetic_pathways">Synthetic genetic pathways</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=14" title="Edit section: Synthetic genetic pathways">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Traditional metabolic engineering has been bolstered by the introduction of combinations of foreign genes and optimization by directed evolution. Perhaps the best known application of synthetic biology to date is engineering <i><a href="/wiki/E._coli" title="E. coli" class="mw-redirect">E. coli</a></i> and <a href="/wiki/Yeast" title="Yeast">yeast</a> for commercial production of a precursor of the antimalarial drug, <a href="/wiki/Artemisinin" title="Artemisinin">Artemisinin</a>, by the laboratory of <a href="/wiki/Jay_Keasling" title="Jay Keasling">Jay Keasling</a>.<sup class="noprint Inline-Template Template-Fact" style="white-space:nowrap;">[<i><a href="/wiki/Wikipedia:Citation_needed" title="Wikipedia:Citation needed"><span title="This claim needs references to reliable sources. (January 2015)">citation needed</span></a></i>]</sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unnatural_nucleotides">Unnatural nucleotides</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=15" title="Edit section: Unnatural nucleotides">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main articles: <a href="/wiki/Nucleic_acid_analogue" title="Nucleic acid analogue">Nucleic acid analogue</a> and <a href="/wiki/Xenobiology" title="Xenobiology">Xenobiology</a></div>
<p>Many technologies have been developed for incorporating unnatural nucleotides and amino acids into nucleic acids and proteins, both in vitro and in vivo. For example, in May 2014, researchers announced that they had successfully introduced two new artificial <a href="/wiki/Nucleotides" title="Nucleotides" class="mw-redirect">nucleotides</a> into bacterial DNA, and by including individual artificial nucleotides in the culture media, were able to passage the bacteria 24 times; they did not create mRNA or proteins able to use the artificial nucleotides.<sup id="cite_ref-NYT-20140507_33-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NYT-20140507-33"><span>[</span>33<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NATURE-20140507_34-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NATURE-20140507-34"><span>[</span>34<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-NATJ-20140507_35-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-NATJ-20140507-35"><span>[</span>35<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Unnatural_amino_acids">Unnatural amino acids</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=16" title="Edit section: Unnatural amino acids">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<div class="hatnote relarticle mainarticle">Main article: <a href="/wiki/Expanded_genetic_code" title="Expanded genetic code">Expanded genetic code</a></div>
<p>Another common topic of investigation is expansion of the normal repertoire of 20 <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>. Excluding <a href="/wiki/Stop_codon" title="Stop codon">stop codons</a>, there are 61 <a href="/wiki/Codons" title="Codons" class="mw-redirect">codons</a>, but only 20 amino acids are coded in virtually all organisms. Certain codons are engineered to code for an alternative amino acid, including nonstandard (such as O-methyl <a href="/wiki/Tyrosine" title="Tyrosine">tyrosine</a>) or exogenous (such as 4-fluorophenylalanine) amino acids. Typically, these projects make use of re-coded <a href="/wiki/Nonsense_suppressor" title="Nonsense suppressor">nonsense suppressor</a> <a href="/wiki/Transfer_RNA" title="Transfer RNA">tRNA</a>-<a href="/wiki/Aminoacyl_tRNA_synthetase" title="Aminoacyl tRNA synthetase">Aminoacyl tRNA synthetase</a> pairs from other organisms, though in most cases substantial engineering is still required.<sup id="cite_ref-36" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-36"><span>[</span>36<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Reduced_amino-acid_libraries">Reduced amino-acid libraries</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=17" title="Edit section: Reduced amino-acid libraries">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>Instead of expanding the genetic code, other researchers have investigated the structure and function of proteins by reducing the normal set of 20 <a href="/wiki/Amino_acid" title="Amino acid">amino acids</a>, that is, by generating proteins where certain groups of amino acids may be substituted with a single amino acid.<sup id="cite_ref-37" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-37"><span>[</span>37<span>]</span></a></sup> For instance, several <a href="/wiki/Chemical_polarity" title="Chemical polarity">non-polar</a> amino acids within a protein may all be replaced with a single non-polar amino acid.<sup id="cite_ref-38" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-38"><span>[</span>38<span>]</span></a></sup> One project demonstrated that an engineered version of <a href="/wiki/Chorismate_mutase" title="Chorismate mutase">Chorismate mutase</a> still had catalytic activity when only 9 amino acids were used.<sup id="cite_ref-39" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-39"><span>[</span>39<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Designed_proteins">Designed proteins</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=18" title="Edit section: Designed proteins">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>While there are methods to engineer natural proteins (such as by <a href="/wiki/Directed_evolution" title="Directed evolution">Directed evolution</a>), there are also projects to design novel protein structures that match or improve on the functionality of existing proteins. One group generated a <a href="/wiki/Helix_bundle" title="Helix bundle">helix bundle</a> that was capable of binding <a href="/wiki/Oxygen" title="Oxygen">oxygen</a> with similar properties as <a href="/wiki/Hemoglobin" title="Hemoglobin">hemoglobin</a>, yet did not bind <a href="/wiki/Carbon_monoxide" title="Carbon monoxide">carbon monoxide</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-40" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-40"><span>[</span>40<span>]</span></a></sup> A similar protein structure was generated to support a variety of <a href="/wiki/Oxidoreductase" title="Oxidoreductase">oxidoreductase</a> activities.<sup id="cite_ref-41" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-41"><span>[</span>41<span>]</span></a></sup> Another group generated a family of G-protein coupled receptors which could be activated by the inert small molecule clozapine-N-oxide but insensitive to the native ligand (acetylcholine)<sup id="cite_ref-42" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-42"><span>[</span>42<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Biosensors">Biosensors</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=19" title="Edit section: Biosensors">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>A <a href="/wiki/Biosensor" title="Biosensor">biosensor</a> refers to an engineered organism (usually a bacterium) that is capable of reporting some environmental phenomenon, such the presence of heavy metals or toxins. In this respect, a very widely used system is the <a href="/w/index.php?title=Lux_operon&amp;action=edit&amp;redlink=1" class="new" title="Lux operon (page does not exist)">Lux operon</a> of <i><a href="/wiki/Aliivibrio_fischeri" title="Aliivibrio fischeri">Aliivibrio fischeri</a></i>. The Lux operon consists of five genes which are <a href="/wiki/Necessity_and_sufficiency" title="Necessity and sufficiency">necessary and sufficient</a> for bacterial <a href="/wiki/Bioluminescence" title="Bioluminescence">bioluminescence</a>, and can be placed under an alternate <a href="/wiki/Promoter_(genetics)" title="Promoter (genetics)">promoter</a> to express the genes in response to an arbitrary environmental stimulus. One such sensor created in <a href="/wiki/Oak_Ridge_National_Laboratory" title="Oak Ridge National Laboratory">Oak Ridge National Laboratory</a> and named “critter on a chip” used a coating of <a href="/wiki/Bioluminescent_bacteria" title="Bioluminescent bacteria">bioluminescent bacteria</a> on a light sensitive <a href="/wiki/Computer_chip" title="Computer chip" class="mw-redirect">computer chip</a> to detect certain <a href="/wiki/Petroleum" title="Petroleum">petroleum</a> <a href="/wiki/Pollutant" title="Pollutant">pollutants</a>. When the bacteria sense the pollutant, they begin to generate light.<sup id="cite_ref-43" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-43"><span>[</span>43<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Materials_production">Materials production</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=20" title="Edit section: Materials production">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>By integrating synthetic biology approaches with <a href="/wiki/Materials_sciences" title="Materials sciences" class="mw-redirect">materials sciences</a>, it would be possible to envision cells as microscopic molecular foundries to produce materials with properties that can be genetically encoded. Recent advances towards this include reengineering curli fibers, the <a href="/wiki/Amyloid" title="Amyloid">amyloid</a> component of extracellular material of <a href="/wiki/Biofilms" title="Biofilms" class="mw-redirect">biofilms</a>, as a platform for a programmable <a href="/wiki/Nanomaterial" title="Nanomaterial" class="mw-redirect">nanomaterial</a>. These nanofibers have been genetically programmed for specific functions, including adhesion to substrates, nanoparticle templating, and protein immobilization.<sup id="cite_ref-44" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-44"><span>[</span>44<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h2><span class="mw-headline" id="Bioethics_and_security_issues">Bioethics and security issues</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=21" title="Edit section: Bioethics and security issues">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h2>
<p>In addition to numerous scientific and technical challenges, synthetic biology raises ethical issues and biosecurity issues. However, with the exception of regulating DNA synthesis companies,<sup id="cite_ref-B.C3.BCgl.2C_H._et_al._2007_627.E2.80.93629_45-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B.C3.BCgl.2C_H._et_al._2007_627.E2.80.93629-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup> the issues are not seen as new because they were raised during the earlier <a href="/wiki/Recombinant_DNA" title="Recombinant DNA">recombinant DNA</a> and <a href="/wiki/Genetically_modified_organism" title="Genetically modified organism">genetically modified organism</a> (GMO) debates and there were already extensive regulations of <a href="/wiki/Genetic_engineering" title="Genetic engineering">genetic engineering</a> and pathogen research in place in the U.S.A., Europe and the rest of the world.<sup id="cite_ref-bioethics.gov_46-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bioethics.gov-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>The European Union funded project SYNBIOSAFE<sup id="cite_ref-47" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-47"><span>[</span>47<span>]</span></a></sup> has issued several reports on how to manage the risks of synthetic biology. A 2007 paper identified key issues in the areas of safety, security, ethics and the science-society interface (the latter of which they defined as public education and as ongoing dialogue among scientists, businesses, government, and ethicists).<sup id="cite_ref-Priorities_48-0" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-Priorities-48"><span>[</span>48<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-49" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-49"><span>[</span>49<span>]</span></a></sup> Key security issues involved engaging companies that sell synthetic DNA and the <a href="/wiki/Biohacking" title="Biohacking">Biohacking</a> community of amateur biologists. Key ethical issues concerned the creation of new life forms. A subsequent report focused on biosecurity issues, especially the so-called <a href="/wiki/Dual_use_technology" title="Dual use technology" class="mw-redirect">dual-use</a> challenge. For example, while the study of synthetic biology may lead to more efficient ways to produce medical treatments (e.g. against malaria, see <a href="/wiki/Artemisinin#Synthesis_in_engineered_organisms" title="Artemisinin">artemisinin</a>), it may also lead to synthesis or redesign of harmful pathogens (e.g., smallpox) by malicious actors.<sup id="cite_ref-50" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-50"><span>[</span>50<span>]</span></a></sup> The bio-hacking community remains a source of special concern, as the distributed and diffuse nature of open-source biotechnology makes it difficult to track, regulate, or mitigate potential biosafety and biosecurity concerns.<sup id="cite_ref-51" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-51"><span>[</span>51<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>COSY is another European initiative - its focus is on public perception and communication of synthetic biology.<sup id="cite_ref-52" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-52"><span>[</span>52<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-53" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-53"><span>[</span>53<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-54" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-54"><span>[</span>54<span>]</span></a></sup> To better communicate synthetic biology and its societal ramifications to a broader public, COSY and SYNBIOSAFE published a 38-minute documentary film in October 2009.<sup id="cite_ref-55" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-55"><span>[</span>55<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>An initiative for self-regulation has been proposed by the International Association Synthetic Biology<sup id="cite_ref-56" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-56"><span>[</span>56<span>]</span></a></sup> that suggests some specific measures to be implemented by the synthetic biology industry, especially DNA synthesis companies. In 2007, a group led by scientists from leading DNA synthesis companies published a "practical plan for developing an effective oversight framework for the DNA-synthesis industry."<sup id="cite_ref-B.C3.BCgl.2C_H._et_al._2007_627.E2.80.93629_45-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-B.C3.BCgl.2C_H._et_al._2007_627.E2.80.93629-45"><span>[</span>45<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>In January 2009, the <a href="/wiki/Alfred_P._Sloan_Foundation" title="Alfred P. Sloan Foundation">Alfred P. Sloan Foundation</a> funded the <a href="/wiki/Woodrow_Wilson_Center" title="Woodrow Wilson Center" class="mw-redirect">Woodrow Wilson Center</a>, the <a href="/wiki/Hastings_Center" title="Hastings Center" class="mw-redirect">Hastings Center</a>, and the <a href="/wiki/J._Craig_Venter_Institute" title="J. Craig Venter Institute">J. Craig Venter Institute</a> to examine the public perception, ethics, and policy implications of synthetic biology.<sup id="cite_ref-57" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-57"><span>[</span>57<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>On July 9–10, 2009, the National Academies' Committee of Science, Technology &amp; Law convened a symposium on "Opportunities and Challenges in the Emerging Field of Synthetic Biology".<sup id="cite_ref-58" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-58"><span>[</span>58<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<p>After the publication of the <a href="/wiki/Mycoplasma_laboratorium" title="Mycoplasma laboratorium">first synthetic genome</a> by <a href="/wiki/Craig_Venter" title="Craig Venter">Craig Venter</a>'s group and the accompanying media coverage about "life" being created, President Obama requested the <a href="/wiki/Presidential_Commission_for_the_Study_of_Bioethical_Issues" title="Presidential Commission for the Study of Bioethical Issues">Presidential Commission for the Study of Bioethical Issues</a> to study synthetic biology.<sup id="cite_ref-59" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-59"><span>[</span>59<span>]</span></a></sup> The commission convened a series of meetings, then issued a report in December 2010 titled "New Directions: The Ethics of Synthetic Biology and Emerging Technologies." The report clarified that the Venter group had not created life, and noted that synthetic biology is an emerging field, which creates potential risks and rewards. The commission did not recommend any changes to policy or oversight and called for continued funding of the research and new funding for monitoring, study of emerging ethical issues, and public education.<sup id="cite_ref-bioethics.gov_46-1" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-bioethics.gov-46"><span>[</span>46<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
<h3><span class="mw-headline" id="Opposition_to_synthetic_biology">Opposition to synthetic biology</span><span class="mw-editsection"><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">[</span><a href="/w/index.php?title=Synthetic_biology&amp;action=edit&amp;section=22" title="Edit section: Opposition to synthetic biology">edit</a><span class="mw-editsection-bracket">]</span></span></h3>
<p>On March 13, 2012, over 100 environmental and civil society groups, including <a href="/wiki/Friends_of_the_Earth" title="Friends of the Earth">Friends of the Earth</a>, the <a href="/wiki/International_Center_for_Technology_Assessment" title="International Center for Technology Assessment">International Center for Technology Assessment</a> and the <a href="/wiki/ETC_Group_(AGETC)" title="ETC Group (AGETC)">ETC Group</a> issued the manifesto <i>The Principles for the Oversight of Synthetic Biology</i> which call for a worldwide moratorium on the release and commercial use of synthetic organisms until more robust regulations and rigorous biosafety measures are established. The groups specifically call for an outright ban on the use of synthetic biology on the <a href="/wiki/Human_genome" title="Human genome">human genome</a> or <a href="/wiki/Human_microbiome" title="Human microbiome" class="mw-redirect">human microbiome</a>.<sup id="cite_ref-60" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-60"><span>[</span>60<span>]</span></a></sup><sup id="cite_ref-61" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-61"><span>[</span>61<span>]</span></a></sup> <a href="/wiki/Richard_Lewontin" title="Richard Lewontin">Richard Lewontin</a> wrote that some of the safety tenets for oversight discussed in <i>The Principles for the Oversight of Synthetic Biology</i> are reasonable, but that the main problem with the recommendations in the manifesto is that "the public at large lacks the ability to enforce any meaningful realization of those recommendations."<sup id="cite_ref-62" class="reference"><a href="#cite_note-62"><span>[</span>62<span>]</span></a></sup></p>
