                <h1><a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/uncategorized/is-there-molecular-cell-biology-in-asu-bme/">Is There Molecular Cell Biology in ASU BME?</a> </h1>
                                <div class="recover"></div>
            </div>
			
			<div class="entry">
				<p>Dear Biomedical Engineering (BME) First-year Student,</p>
<p>As you know, the first year in a college program is a very critical year. You don&#8217;t have long to make a decision about which major(s) to seal a ~4-year long commitment with. You may have spoken with peers, second-year students and beyond, or professors at ASU and heard about all of the mathematical, programming, computer, and hardware design classes that are required in the BME curriculum. You may have heard some people comment that BME does not offer much for those interested in genetic engineering, molecular cell biology, synthetic biology, and other fields related to studying and manipulating DNA, proteins, stem cells, antibodies, etc.</p>
<p>A bit worried, you may have approached me or other BME faculty and asked <em><strong>&#8220;should I stay in BME or switch to the School of Life Sciences (SOLS)?&#8221;</strong></em> You may have received different answers.</p>
<p>I am writing this open letter to give you some helpful and important insights into BME. The molecular cell biology content offered in the BME major may not be obvious after just skimming the course titles. However, the content is indeed there and with the hiring of new faculty in Molecular Cell and Tissue Bioengineering and Synthetic Biology, the content is growing. Importantly, the molecular cell biology content offered by BME is presented in a way that makes the medical applications much more obvious than most other courses offered by other programs. If medical context appeals to you, I personally encourage you to stick with BME.</p>
<p><em><strong>1. Start Planning Now to Take the Following Courses</strong></em></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>BME100 &#8211; Introduction to Biomedical Engineering</strong> (Lecture and Lab)<br />Offered: Fall, Spring<br />Offered by: Profs. <a href="http://faculty.engineering.asu.edu/garcia/" target="_blank">Garcia</a>, <a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/" target="_blank">Haynes</a>, <a href="http://sbhse.engineering.asu.edu/faculty-and-staff/faculty/jeff-kleim/" target="_blank">Kleim</a> <br />You will learn about: <strong>Cell engineering, gene delivery, gene networks, DNA analysis</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>BME318 &#8211; Biomaterials</strong> (Lecture and Lab)<br />Offered: Fall, Spring<br />Offered by: Prof. <a href="http://stabenfeldt.lab.asu.edu/" target="_blank">Stabenfeldt</a>, Dr. Ankeny<br />You will learn about: <strong>Protein interactions, cell adhesion/ scaffolding, inflammatory response, immune response, infection</strong></p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>BME331 &#8211; Mass Transport</strong> <br />Offered: Fall, Spring<br />Offered by: Prof. <a href="http://faculty.engineering.asu.edu/caplan/" target="_blank">Caplan</a> <br />You will learn about: Biomolecules, cell transport</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>BME494/598 &#8211; Molecular Synthetic Biology</strong> <br />Offered: Spring (even years)<br />Offered by: Prof. <a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/" target="_blank">Haynes</a> <br />You will learn about: Synthetic genes, molecular cloning, gene delivery, gene &amp; protein design</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"><strong>The International Genetically Engineered Machine Competition (<a href="http://igem.org/Main_Page" target="_blank">iGEM</a>)</strong><br />Not a course, but a spin-out of BME494/598. One of the student-designed projects developed in the course will be offered support (lab space, materials, iGEM registration fees, travel). The project will be completed over the following summer. The international conference/ competition occurs in early Fall.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;">&#8230;And several courses for MS and PhD graduate students (500-level and above)</p>
<p style="padding-left: 30px;"> </p>
<p><em><strong>2. Consider Adding a Life Sciences Class to Your Curriculum</strong></em></p>
<p>Find a biology course that has a strong <span style="text-decoration: underline;"><em>quantitative</em></span> emphasis (e.g., bioinformatics, protein engineering, biochemistry). If you are going to engineer biological systems, you will need to learn how math and computer software is used to understand biology, in addition to the basic concepts and some wet lab techniques. Discuss with your academic advisor how to fit one or a few of these classes into your curriculum. <em><span style="text-decoration: underline;">Do not over-commit</span></em>&#8230;prioritize the classes that fit your interests.</p>
<p><em><strong>3. Plan to Secure a Position in a Wet Lab</strong></em></p>
<p>BME professors run research labs when they are not teaching. Visit the BME faculty page to find links to their labs&#8217; websites. Find one or more that you are interested in. Send them an e-mail and ask if they have any room for undergraduate volunteers. Over time while you are volunteering, if you find that the lab is a good fit write up an idea for a <a href="http://more.engineering.asu.edu/furi/" target="_blank">FURI proposal</a>.</p>
<p><em><strong>4. Keep Track of Changes in the Course Offerings</strong></em></p>
<p>ASU is growing very quickly. Almost every year, a new faculty joins a department here, including SBHSE (BME). Look out for new SBHSE faculty who focus on molecular cell biology and find out what classes they are offering. This information can be easily accessed by looking up that faculty&#8217;s directory profile page.</p>
<p>&nbsp;</p>
<p>I hope that you find this bit of advice helpful. All the best on your career path, wherever it may take you.</p>
