        <h2 class="home"><a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/uncategorized/haynes-lab-receives-grant-from-women-and-philanthropy/">Haynes Receives Grant from Women and Philanthropy</a> </h2>                <div class="recover"></div>
    </div>

				
    <div class="entry_content">
        <p><a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/BNJ_0440.jpg"><img class="alignnone  wp-image-528" title="Women&amp;Philanthropy1" src="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/wp-content/uploads/2014/06/BNJ_0440.jpg" alt="" width="500" /></a></p>
<p>Getting a grant is extremely exciting, especially for a young PI who has to find support after she has exhausted her start-up funds. In addition to receiving new funding, something else truly amazing happened. My science-communication skills were challenged like they had never been before. I got a very broad audience excited about synthetic biology&#8230;something that I believed only scientists and students could ever truly get excited about. The Women and Philanthropy grant application experience was worlds apart from what I am used to&#8230;scientifically convincing a panel of scientists that I am a scientist worth her salt who can science, science, science, and science. Instead, I had to identify the real impact that my work would have on my University, community, and the world; how stakeholders who are often taken for granted (the public) would benefit from the work.</p>
<p>I first put pen to paper to materialize my ideas for enabling distributed characterization of bioenginered parts at the 2012 Synthetic Biology Leadership Excellence Accelerator Program (SynBioLEAP), held in Arlington, VA (<a href="http://synbioleap.org/visions-and-plans/">SynBioLEAP White Paper &#8211; &#8220;Incentive-Driven Information Sharing for Engineering Biology&#8221;</a>). Since then I have been seeking out support and collaborators. After an unsuccessful attempt to get the project funded by the NSF as part of a Career proposal, I tried <strong>another route</strong>.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 60px;">Women have an unconventional approach to science that is not always supported by funding agencies with less broad views on how science and research should be approached.</p>
<p style="padding-left: 60px;"><em>Dr. Peggy Rismiller</em></p>
<p>I sought out a collaboration with Catherine Seiler, curator of the amazing DNA plasmid repository <a href="https://dnasu.org/DNASU/">DNASU</a>, which is just across the street from my building on Arizona State University&#8217;s Tempe campus. A few brainstorming sessions later the blueprint for &#8220;SB.ASU&#8221; (Synthetic Biology at ASU) was born. We formed a three-woman team, along with my graduate student Rene Davis, to apply for support from Women and Philanthropy.</p>
<p>The project aims take DNA parts repositories to a new level. We will develop a tool that gathers real experiences from research labs to describe how those parts function in living cells. Bioengineering needs functional data to move forward with rational design of new technologies to build new tools for biomedical and energy needs. We anticipate that the project will reduce the time, cost, and guesswork involved in bioengineering. Read more about the project in <a href="http://fullcircle.asu.edu/2014/05/10501/" target="_blank">ASU Full Circle&#8217;s article</a> by Joe Kullman.</p>
<p>Related articles<br /> 1. ASU full Circle. Haynes looks to create a global resource for synthetic biology researchers. <a href="http://fullcircle.asu.edu/2014/05/10501/">http://fullcircle.asu.edu/2014/05/10501/</a></p>

        <h2 class="home"><a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/uncategorized/a-giant-leap-for-synthetic-genes/">A Giant Leap for Synthetic Genes</a> </h2>                	<div class="comment-bubble"><span><a href="http://haynes.lab.asu.edu/uncategorized/a-giant-leap-for-synthetic-genes/#respond" title="Comment on A Giant Leap for Synthetic Genes"><span class="nocomment">No Comment</span></a></span></div>
                <div class="recover"></div>
    </div>

				
    <div class="entry_content">
        <div>Science magazine has just published the latest <em>tour de force</em> in building genetic machinery from the ground-up since <em>Mycoplasma laboratorium</em> (&#8220;Mycoplasma of the laboratory&#8221;), dubbed <em>Synthia </em>by some. <em>M. lab</em>, the &#8220;synthetic cell,&#8221; represented a major milestone in the synthesis of functional DNA, which was announced just four years ago [1]. Craig Venter&#8217;s group from the JCVI had reported the complete chemical synthesis of a chromosome that was able to support living, bacterial <em>Mycoplasma genitalium</em> cells.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>This time in 2014, a different team of scientists synthesized a chromosome that can support living <em><strong>yeast</strong></em> cells [2]. The ultimate goal of the  Synthetic Yeast 2.0 project (<a href="http://syntheticyeast.org" target="_blank">http://syntheticyeast.org</a>) is to build the entire genetic make-up of a yeast cell and use it to boot-up <em>Sc2.0</em>. How ambitious is this endeavor? For reference, <em>M. lab</em> is a tiny bacterium that measures about 0.1 micrometer across and carries 0.58 million letters, or nucleotides (A&#8217;s, T&#8217;s, C&#8217;s, and G&#8217;s), in its complete genetic code. Sc2.0 will measure a whopping 10 micrometers across (the width of 100 <em>M. labs</em>). The chromosome that the yeast team built, chromosome number 3, consists of 0.27 million letters. All together, the genetic code of <em>Sc2.0</em> may add up to 14 million letters.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>This accomplishment has major implications for human cell engineering, and for understanding life. Compared to mycoplasma, the internal organization of yeast cells more closely matches human cells. Furthermore, the yeast team added many unique, synthetic features to the yeast chromosome and demonstrated that this chromosome could still support life. The function of the new features is to aid the process of clipping out portions of the chromosome at specific sites in order to discover what happens to yeast after you eliminate presumably &#8220;non-essential&#8221; stuff.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>This demonstration that a huge synthetic chromosome can reside peacefully amongst the other 15 native chromosomes inside a <em><strong>eukaryotic</strong></em> cell is a major milestone for synthetic biology. We (humans) are also <strong>eukaryotes</strong>. The yeast team&#8217;s work is an important step for advancing human cell engineering. The work helps to determine to what extent a eukaryote will tolerate artificial genetic &#8220;plug-ins&#8221;. Now that the size limit is bigger than ever reported, we can build more complex and more useful synthetic systems. The work will also help us to finally answer the vexing question of whether our &#8220;junk&#8221; DNA is essential for life, or perhaps just an evolutionary artifact.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>Very impressive work scientifically, but what moves me the most is the personality of the endeavor. Jef Boeke had organized this effort around an undergraduate lab course called &#8220;Build-a-Genome&#8221; (or BAG) to give college students an authentic graduate-level research experience [3]. Students were assigned chunks of the synthetic chromosome to build. I can only imagine how amazed they must be to see the impact that the end-product has had on basic research and synthetic biology.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>The international yeast team includes scientists from Johns Hopkins University, The Carnegie Institution of Washington, New York University, and Loyola University in the <strong>USA</strong>, Institut Pasteur and Université Pierre et Marie Curie in <strong>France</strong>, the University of Edinburgh in <strong>Scotland</strong>, and Pondicherry Biotech in <strong>India</strong>. One of the leaders of this effort, <a href="http://www.mbg.jhmi.edu/Pages/people/profile.aspx?PID=2" target="_blank">Jef Boeke</a>, was interviewed on <a href="http://www.sciencefriday.com/guests/jef-boeke.html#page/full-width-list/1" target="_blank">NPR&#8217;s Science Friday</a> [4]. Yizhi &#8220;Patrick&#8221; Cai, who happens to be an alum of the widely popular <a href="http://igem.org/Main_Page" target="_blank">International Genetically Engineered Machines Competition (iGEM)</a>, was recently awarded a grant of 1.8 million pounds to establish the new Edinburgh Genome Foundry.</div>
<div> </div>
<div>Congratulations team yeast! Only 15 more chromosomes to go&#8230;</div>

<div>2. Annaluru, N., Muller, H., Mitchell, L. A., Ramalingam, S., Stracquadanio, G., Richardson, S. M., et al. (2014). Total Synthesis of a Functional Designer Eukaryotic Chromosome. Science. doi:10.1126/science.1249252</div>

                <div class="recover"></div>
    </div>

				
    <div class="entry_content">
        <p>We are recruiting new members for the <strong>International Genetically Engineered Machines competition, iGEM</strong>. Never heard of iGEM before? Visit <a href="http://igem.org/About" target="_blank">http://igem.org/About</a> for more information and some examples of award-winning team projects.</p>
<p><strong>What does the ASU iGEM experience look like?</strong></p>
<ul>
<li><strong>Spring</strong> &#8211; meet with your teammates and advisors, develop a project idea, meet other budding teams across the Southwest</li>
<li><strong>Summer</strong> (May &#8211; August) &#8211; use DNA as building blocks to develop a living cell that carries out an engineered function, such as blinking, toggle-switching, generating valuable chemical compounds&#8230;yes, biology can do that. Apply graphic design/ web page building to communicate the team&#8217;s project to the world, scientists, non-scientists, and students. Think forward&#8230;prepare to explain what impact your invention will have on a world in which your synthetic organism has not yet become a part of society.</li>
<li><strong>Fall</strong> (October) &#8211; bring your results to the most exciting student research jamboree in the world, which takes place in Boston, MA. Return to ASU as celebrated heroes of bioengineering!</li>
</ul>
<p>We are seeking undergraduate applicants with a minimum of some coursework that is relevant to synthetic biology (e.g., biology, molecular biology, circuit design, applied math/ calculus, relevant humanities course, graphic design, etc.).</p>
<p>You should definitely consider registering for Dr. Haynes&#8217; course <em><strong>Molecular Synthetic Biology</strong></em>, or at least attend our research article discussions.</p>
<p>DEADLINE EXTENSION: Please <strong><a href="https://docs.google.com/a/asu.edu/forms/d/1vQ0mvFzThkUyDjRHAAOLAJGF3GOMn_Gyr08TAtyWAv0/viewform" target="_blank">complete the application online</a></strong> by Friday, February 21st. Send inquiries to the lead faculty advisor Dr. Haynes. DO NOT send your c.v./ resume/ or transcript at this time. We may ask for these items later in the selection process. We will follow up in early February and hold an in-person, group interview of the finalists in late February/ early March.</p>
<p>If you are not an undergraduate but want to get involved, please e-mail Dr. Karmella Haynes.</p>

The project is being undertaken by the national Synthetic Biology Engineering Research Center (SynBERC), which is supported by the National Science Foundation (NSF).
