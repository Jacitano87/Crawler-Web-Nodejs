<h3><a href="http://syntheticbiology.web.unc.edu/?p=18" rel="bookmark" title="What is Synthetic Biology?">What is Synthetic Biology?</a></h3>
<div class="contenttext">
<div>
<ul>
<li>“I believe that the capacity to synthesize and construct DNA is currently the number one <strong>most important technology of the 21st century</strong>,” Drew Endy, bioengineer at Stanford University, director at BIOFAB International (qtd. in Hsu 2010a).</li>
<li>You should be able to make cells do and produce things that nothing in nature has ever done or produced before (Mooallem 2010).</li>
</ul>
<div id="_mcePaste" style="text-align: center;"><em>Synthetic Biology Concept Explained (May 28, 2008)</em></div>
<div id="_mcePaste" style="text-align: center;"><span class="vvqbox vvqyoutube" style="width:425px;height:344px;"><span id="vvq-18-youtube-1"><a href="http://www.youtube.com/watch?v=VG1Ia115K0c"><img src="http://img.youtube.com/vi/VG1Ia115K0c/0.jpg" alt="YouTube Preview Image" /></a></span></span></div>
<ul>
<li>Synthetic biology is the production or redesign of biological systems via the generation of novel DNA segments absent in nature (Hsu 2010b; NSF 2010).</li>
<li>The de novo synthesis of DNA sequences, may used to produce biological parts, or biological components, such as: enzymes, genetic circuits, and metabolic pathways. The assembly of these parts may permit the creation of organisms capable of performing functions not necessarily found in nature (Keasling 2008; Katsnelson 2010; NSF 2010).</li>
</ul>
